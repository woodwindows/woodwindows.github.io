The Inside Out Window

When constructing a casement sash, you have the choice of prioritizing a particular set of dimensions. You can either concentrate on getting the exterior of the window to be your target size or you can prioritize getting the interior of the window to match your target size.

Obviously, if everything is measured to high accuracy, material is correctly sized, everything is cut square, and there are no mistakes, this prioritization does not matter and the window you produce with either philosophy will be exactly the same. But life isn't perfect, and prioritizing everything is prioritizing nothing, so decide which is more important:

  1. the exterior of the casement (perhaps you need to match an opening in an existing frame) or
  2. the interior of the casement (perhaps you are constrained by already having toughened glass ordered to spec dimensions)

I've chosen to prioritize the interior measurements for a consistent glass size: The Inside Out Window

Mortice Distance & Shoulder Distance

The interior size of the window is determined by 2 distances: the mortice distance and the shoulder distance.

The mortice distance is the interior distance between the mortices in the stile.

The shoulder distance is the distance between the shoulders of the rail.

So it is the inner edge of the mortices and the locations of the shoulders that should be marked on the stiles and rails first and further marks based on these.

These marks are essential for prioritizing the interior measurements, but they also give you the freedom to keep the stile and rail overlong in a natural way - the ends neither need to be square, nor do the pieces need to be exactly the same length - because you don't ever use the end of the pieces as reference.

The window design that I'm using actually has two shoulders at each end of the rail at different positions. When talking about the shoulder distance, I'm referring to the shoulders that are closer to the end of the rail. The position of the inner shoulder is only a consequence of the depth of the rebate and should be viewed as subordinate, not as a primary landmark. The shoulder that counts is the one that you can see on the room-side, front face of the casement, not the one you can see from the world-side, back face of the casement.

A consequence is that, during fitting, if the front shoulder has a gap and the back shoulder does not, the back shoulder should be eased to close the gap resulting in no gaps on either side. On the other hand, if there is no gap at the front shoulder, but there is a gap at the back, the front shoulder should not be eased to allow a close fit at the back if the front shoulder is already correctly positioned.

Sticks

The mortice and shoulder distances are important enough that it is worth creating sticks of these lengths to speed marking and aid later size checks. It can be useful to make these from the pieces cut from the rebates, but any spare material can be used as long as it is is straight and will preserve its length for the life of the project. The ends of the sticks should be clean and square to the sides so that marks can be struck against the ends and there is no doubt about what distance is defined. The sticks should be clearly and redundantly labeled as to their size & purpose. You may also choose to create a rod for the window containing all relevant measurements, but being able to confirm that a window has the correct internal dimensions during dry fitting just by placing a stick in the opening is very useful which you cannot do only do when the entire length matches the distance you wish to check.

Face & Edge

The face of our window - the first surface we use for measurements - is the room side surface of the window - the side that is on display when the window is in its normal, closed position.

The edge of the window - the second surface we use for measurements - is the interior edge of the stiles and rails.
