The Inside Out Window

When constructing a casement sash, you have the choice of prioritizing a particular set of dimensions. You can either concentrate on getting the exterior of the window to be your target size or you can prioritize getting the interior of the window to match your target size.

Obviously, if everything is measured to high accuracy, material is correctly sized, everything is cut square, and there are no mistakes, this prioritization does not matter and the window you produce with either philosophy will be exactly the same. But life isn't perfect, and prioritizing everything is prioritizing nothing, so decide which is more important:

  1. the exterior of the casement (perhaps you need to match an opening in an existing frame) or
  2. the interior of the casement (perhaps you are constrained by already having toughened glass ordered to spec dimensions)

I've chosen to prioritize the interior measurements for a consistent glass size and I call this "The Inside Out Window"

First, we need to recognize which features of the casement determine the size of the glass.

Vertically, the size is determined by the distance between the bottom-to-top (interior) distance between the mortices on the stile.

In some designs, it's also determined by the offset between the glazing rebate and the tenon, but I'm using a design where the tenon and rebate edges are at the same position so there is no offset and no distinction between tenon and rebate.

Horizontally, the size is determined by the distance between the tenon shoulders at each end of the rail.

This design has two shoulders at each end of the rail at different positions. We're talking about the shoulder that is closer to the end of the rail and the distance between these at either end of the rail. The position of the inner shoulder is only a consequence of the depth of the rebate and should be viewed as subordinate, not as a primary landmark. The shoulder that counts is the one that you can see on the room-side, front face of the casement, not the one you can see from the world-side, back face of the casement.
